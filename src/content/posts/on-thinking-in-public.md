There's a version of writing where you wait until the thought is complete — fully formed, airtight, every implication considered — and only then commit it to a page. I used to do this. The result was that I published very little and kept an enormous private document of half-finished thinking that never became anything.

The other way is to publish the sketch.

## The sketch is the work

What I mean by sketch: an essay that's mostly there but doesn't have every corner resolved. An argument that's directionally right even if some specific claims could be picked apart. Thinking that is genuinely exploratory rather than declarative.

The risk is obvious — you look uncertain, even confused. You might be wrong in public. Someone might point out you've missed the obvious counterargument that sits in paragraph two of the Wikipedia article.

But here's what I've found over time: the sketch is usually *more* interesting than the finished thing. The finished thing has had all the rough edges sanded off. It's lost the quality of someone actually wrestling with an idea, which is the quality that makes writing worth reading.

> The best nonfiction writers I admire are not the ones with all the answers. They're the ones asking the questions most precisely.

## What happens when you publish the sketch

Two things, mostly.

First: you find out what you actually think. Writing for an audience — even a small one — forces a kind of clarity that writing privately doesn't. When there are readers, you can't hide behind vagueness. You have to commit, even provisionally.

Second: people finish the thought for you. Some of the best ideas I've had have come from replies, messages, or conversations sparked by something I wrote that was clearly unresolved. The essay becomes a starting point for collaborative thinking rather than a monologue.

## The uncomfortable middle

There's still discomfort in all this. I don't think you can fully engineer away the feeling of exposure that comes from publishing something you're not totally sure about.

But I've come to think that's the point. If writing doesn't feel a little exposed, it's probably not saying anything. The most comfortable writing is the most forgettable — the kind that stays exactly within what you already know, confirms what the reader already believes, and takes no risks.

The sketches that make me nervous are usually the ones people write back about.
